# Carousel 03: The AI Liability Chain Is Settling in Court

---

## Slide 1 (Cover)

**HEADLINE:** The AI Liability Chain Is Settling in Court

**Body:** Courts are deciding who pays when AI code causes harm. The answer is forming faster than most companies realize.

---

## Slide 2

**HEADLINE:** The Deployer Eats It: Moffatt v. Air Canada

**Body:** BC Civil Resolution Tribunal held Air Canada liable for its chatbot's misrepresentation. The court rejected the argument that the chatbot was a "separate legal entity." You own what your AI tells the world.

**Source:** Moffatt v. Air Canada, 2024 BCCRT 149 (Feb 2024)

---

## Slide 3

**HEADLINE:** Vendor as Agent: Mobley v. Workday

**Body:** Judge Rita Lin held that an AI vendor can be directly liable as an "agent" under employment law. Certified as collective action July 2025. EEOC filed amicus in support.

**Source:** Mobley v. Workday, 3:23-cv-00770 (N.D. Cal.)

---

## Slide 4

**HEADLINE:** EU AI Act Enforces Aug 2 2026

**Body:** High-risk AI systems face penalties up to 35 million euros or 7% of global revenue. Finland began enforcement in January 2026. The compliance window is closing.

**Source:** EU AI Act, Annex III (Aug 2, 2026 deadline)

---

## Slide 5

**HEADLINE:** "Reasonable Care" Has a Shape Now

**Body:** Courts apply the Learned Hand test: burden of governance is low, probability of harm is high (25.1% vuln rate), magnitude of loss is catastrophic. Shipping AI code without governance is increasingly hard to defend.

**Source:** Learned Hand formula; AppSec Santa 2026 vuln rate

---

## Slide 6

**HEADLINE:** What Reasonable Care Looks Like

**Body:** Signed delegation tokens showing who authorized what. Append-only audit logs proving what happened. Contract-driven policy enforcement. Evidence generated at runtime, not reconstructed after the fact.

---

## Slide 7

**HEADLINE:** The Liability Default Is Set

**Body:** Deploying company is the default bearer. Vendor liability is rising via agency theory. Individual developer exposure is emerging. The only variable is whether you have documented governance.

## POST CAPTION

The AI liability chain is no longer theoretical. Courts are deciding who pays when AI-generated code causes harm.

Moffatt v. Air Canada (2024): the deploying company is liable for its AI's misrepresentations. The court rejected the argument that a chatbot is a separate entity.

Mobley v. Workday (2024-2025): an AI vendor can be held directly liable as an "agent" under employment discrimination law. Certified as a collective action. EEOC filed amicus in support.

EU AI Act: high-risk system penalties up to 35M euros or 7% global revenue, enforcing August 2, 2026. Finland already enforcing since January 2026.

The "reasonable care" standard is forming around documented governance artifacts -- delegation tokens, audit logs, policy enforcement -- generated at runtime, not reconstructed after a breach.

If your company ships AI-assisted code without documented governance, the legal exposure is no longer speculative.
